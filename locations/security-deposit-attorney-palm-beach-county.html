One County, One Law, Every Renter Protected

Palm Beach County spans nearly forty municipalities — West Palm Beach, Boca Raton, Boynton Beach, Delray Beach, Jupiter, Palm Beach Gardens, Wellington, Royal Palm Beach, Lake Worth Beach, Greenacres, Riviera Beach, and dozens more. Rental terms vary wildly across that map, but the law governing your security deposit never changes: Florida Statute 83.49 applies identically in a Jupiter townhome, a Lake Worth Beach cottage, and a Wellington equestrian-community rental.

THE PALM BEACH COUNTY RENTAL PICTURE

A County of Extremes — and a Statute That Doesn’t Care

Few Florida counties cover a wider rental spectrum: oceanfront luxury in Manalapan and Palm Beach, dense workforce housing in Lake Worth Beach and Greenacres, sprawling HOA communities in Wellington and Royal Palm Beach, and agricultural-area rentals out west in Belle Glade and Pahokee. Deposit disputes look different in each — premium “restoration” charges on the coast, silence and no-notice withholding inland — but the statutory analysis is always the same.

Fifteen days to return the deposit in full. Thirty days to claim any deduction by certified mail, itemized and specific. No deductions for ordinary wear and tear. Forfeiture when the landlord fails — and attorney’s fees to the prevailing tenant. Whether your landlord is a Palm Beach trust or a Pahokee individual, that checklist decides the case.

PALM BEACH COUNTY DEPOSIT LAW

How the Law Works in Palm Beach County

The rules are statewide; here’s how they operate across Palm Beach County’s courts and communities.

The 15/30-Day Clock

Anywhere in the county — Jupiter to Boca — a landlord taking no deductions owes your full deposit within 15 days. Any deduction requires an itemized certified-mail claim within 30 days.

Four County Courthouses

Palm Beach County Court hears deposit cases at the Main Courthouse in West Palm Beach and branch courthouses in Delray Beach, Palm Beach Gardens, and Belle Glade. Claims of $8,000 or less go through small claims.

Missed Deadlines = Forfeiture

A landlord who misses the 30-day certified-mail deadline — or mails a vague, lump-sum claim — forfeits the right to keep any of your deposit under Florida law.

The Landlord May Pay Your Fees

When tenants prevail, Florida law generally shifts reasonable attorney’s fees and court costs onto the landlord — the engine behind our no-fee-unless-we-win promise.

PALM BEACH COUNTY DEPOSIT FAQ

Common Questions from Palm Beach County Tenants

What are the deposit deadlines for Palm Beach County landlords?

The same as everywhere in Florida under Statute 83.49: 15 days to return the full deposit when no deductions are taken, or 30 days to send a written, itemized claim by certified mail. A landlord who does neither forfeits the claim and owes the full deposit back.

Which courthouse would handle my case?

Palm Beach County Court operates the Main Courthouse in West Palm Beach plus branch courthouses in Delray Beach (South County), Palm Beach Gardens (North County), and Belle Glade (West County). Filing location follows where the rental sits — we handle that detail, and most cases settle before filing anyway.

Do smaller towns like Lantana, Greenacres, or Pahokee have different rules?

No. Florida Statute 83.49 is state law — no municipality can weaken it. The identical deadlines, wear-and-tear protections, and forfeiture rules apply in every one of the county’s nearly forty municipalities and in unincorporated areas alike.

My landlord kept the deposit for “damages” but never mailed anything. What now?

Silence is the cleanest violation there is. With no certified-mail claim inside 30 days, the landlord has legally forfeited any right to your deposit — whatever the unit’s condition. A demand letter citing the missed deadline, followed by suit if needed, recovers these cases reliably.

How long do I have to bring a Palm Beach County deposit claim?

Florida allows up to five years on claims arising from a written lease, and if the landlord sent a timely itemized notice you have 15 days to object in writing. Older disputes are often still viable — but records fade, so the sooner you call, the stronger the file.
