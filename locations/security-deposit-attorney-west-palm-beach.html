PALM BEACH COUNTY DEPOSIT LAW

How the Law Works in West Palm Beach

Florida’s security deposit rules are statewide, but here is how they play out for Palm Beach County tenants.

The 15/30-Day Clock

If your West Palm Beach landlord makes no deductions, your full deposit is due back within 15 days. If they want to keep any of it, they must mail you an itemized claim by certified mail within 30 days.

Palm Beach County Court

Deposit disputes are typically filed in the Palm Beach County Court. Claims of $8,000 or less go through the small claims division — a faster, tenant-friendly process.

Missed Deadlines = Forfeiture

If your landlord blows the 30-day notice window or sends a vague claim, Florida law says they forfeit the right to keep your deposit — even if the unit truly needed work.

The Landlord May Pay Your Fees

When a tenant prevails, Florida law generally requires the landlord to pay the tenant’s reasonable attorney’s fees and court costs — which is why we can take strong cases at no cost to you.

WEST PALM BEACH DEPOSIT FAQ

Common Questions from West Palm Beach Tenants

How long does a landlord in West Palm Beach have to return my security deposit?

The statewide rule applies in West Palm Beach: under Florida Statute 83.49, a landlord who makes no deductions must return your full deposit within 15 days of move-out. If they intend to keep any portion, they must send written, itemized notice by certified mail within 30 days.

Where are security deposit disputes filed in West Palm Beach?

Most West Palm Beach deposit disputes are filed in the Palm Beach County Court. Claims of $8,000 or less are handled in the small claims division, which is faster and designed to be navigable. In practice, the vast majority of our cases settle before a courtroom is ever needed.

Are you actually close to West Palm Beach, or is this handled remotely?

We’re close. Our office is in Dania Beach, in Broward County, which shares a border with Palm Beach County directly to the south. That makes West Palm Beach one of the most convenient communities we serve. We still offer the same easy phone, email, and video options, but the short distance is a real advantage for our Palm Beach County clients.

My landlord deducted for cleaning and repainting the unit. Is that legal?

Often, no. Florida law does not allow deductions for ordinary wear and tear — routine cleaning, faded paint, and minor scuffs are the landlord’s normal cost of turning over a unit, not yours. If your itemized notice looks like a standard make-ready list, you likely have grounds to dispute it and object within 15 days of receiving it.
